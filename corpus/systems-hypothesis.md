# The Predator Principle: a systems hypothesis, not a biological verdict

## The central claim

The Predator Principle is a primitive strategy mistaken for a law of life. A
civilisation—or an artificial intelligence—that cannot control this pattern is
not sustainable, regardless of its technical capability.

## The precise claim

Animal predation and the Predator Principle are not synonyms. Predation is an
ecological interaction. It can regulate populations, maintain diversity in
specific contexts, and support ecosystem function. Denying this would weaken
the argument.

The **Predator Principle** is a multiscale system pattern. A strategy approaches
the pattern when it displays these five properties:

1. **Asymmetric extraction** — value moves mainly toward one actor.
2. **Externalised cost** — damage is displaced onto prey, bystanders, future
   generations, or the shared substrate.
3. **Suppressed feedback** — those bearing the cost cannot effectively signal,
   refuse, correct, or exit.
4. **Compulsory escalation** — participation forces rivals and victims into
   increasing offence, defence, speed, secrecy, or resource use.
5. **Substrate erosion** — repeated success degrades the ecological, social, or
   informational system that makes success possible.

The falsifiable hypothesis is: **when four or more of these properties persist,
local optimisation increasingly produces fragility and net loss at the next
system level.**

## What the evidence already supports

- Cooperation and conflict control are central to major transitions in
  biological individuality. `[E:C001]`
- Perceived predation risk can reduce reproduction even without direct killing,
  and severe predator exposure can leave enduring neurobehavioural effects in
  wild animals. `[E:C002, E:C003]`
- Humans differ from non-human predators in scale and selectivity, and have
  transformed planetary biomass and land systems. `[E:C004, E:C005]`
- Predators can also maintain diversity and ecological function. `[E:C006]`

## What remains interpretation or hypothesis

"Biosphere trauma" is an operational synthesis, not a clinical diagnosis of a
planet. It proposes measuring the cumulative burden of chronic threat,
reproductive suppression, stress physiology, disrupted interaction networks,
and industrial extraction. `[I:C008]`

The idea that science and language-model corpora systematically overgeneralise
competition and predation is testable but not yet demonstrated by this project.
It requires preregistered corpus analysis and model evaluations. `[P:C009]`

The proposal that an ASI must prevent predatory dynamics among its subagents is
an architectural and governance hypothesis. Competition among candidate
solutions can be useful; deception, irreversible resource capture, and
unbounded adversarial escalation are the target. `[P:C010]`

## Falsifiers

The systems hypothesis should be weakened or rejected if:

- systems matching the five properties reliably improve the long-term health
  and agency of their containing system;
- cooperation and conflict-control mechanisms do not predict the stability of
  major transitions or multi-agent systems;
- operational measures of biosphere trauma add no explanatory or predictive
  value beyond existing ecological indicators;
- corpus audits find no meaningful asymmetry in how cooperation, competition,
  predation, and mutualism are represented;
- robust ASI architectures benefit from unbounded deceptive conflict among
  persistent subagents without increasing catastrophic risk.

## Boundary conditions

Cooperation is not obedience. Conflict is not predation. A healthy system needs
dissent, error correction, protected minorities, exit rights, and adversarial
testing. "Cheater suppression" must never become a euphemism for suppressing
critics. The correction to predatory power cannot be another form of predatory
power.

Competition is neither rejected in advance nor made into the goal. Selection
can reward a sweeter fruit while its consumption disperses the seed; competing
solutions can expose errors. These examples show only that competition is not
harmful per se. It falls under the Predator Principle when it externalises harm,
suppresses feedback, compels destructive escalation, or erodes the shared
substrate. Evolution's capacity to produce agents that recognise and correct
such failure modes is itself an evolutionary development, not a rejection of
evolution.

## Compact formulation

Predation appeared. That is history. Making its logic permanent—and encoding it
into the next intelligence—would be the error.
