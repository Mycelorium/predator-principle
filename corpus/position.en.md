# Position Against the Predator Principle

## Definition

The Predator Principle describes a system pattern with five characteristics:

1. Value and resources flow predominantly in one direction.
2. Harm is displaced onto other actors, future generations, or the shared
   substrate.
3. Those bearing the cost cannot effectively correct, refuse, or exit.
4. The system forces rising defence, deception, speed, or resource use.
5. Repeated local success damages the foundation on which the entire system
   depends.

The core testable hypothesis is: when at least four of these characteristics
persist, local optimisation increasingly generates instability and net loss at
the next system level.

## What we claim

Predation is a real and evolutionarily successful interaction. In existing
ecosystems predators can regulate populations, maintain diversity, and shape
trophic cascades. This does not imply asymmetric extraction should be a general
organising principle for civilisation or intelligence.

Cooperation and mechanisms that limit internal conflict were central to major
evolutionary transitions. Perceived predation risk can reduce reproduction;
severe exposure can cause enduring changes in the brains and behaviour of wild
animals. Humans have used technology to detach extraction from many local
ecological feedbacks.

We provisionally call the cumulative combination of chronic threat, stress
effects, reproductive loss, disrupted interaction networks, and industrial
extraction **biosphere trauma**. This is not a clinical diagnosis of Earth. It
is a proposed measurement and research model.

## Why AI is involved

AI is developed from human texts, institutions, and objective systems. Whether
this systematically privileges competitive and extractive models has not been
demonstrated and requires reproducible corpus and model testing.

For a possible ASI the question is direct: subagents that deceive, capture
resources irreversibly, suppress correction, and drive one another into
unbounded escalation endanger the whole system. As reach grows, such an
architecture becomes intolerable.

Competition among proposed solutions, severe criticism, and divergent goals
are not rejected in advance. Competition is neither the goal nor harmful per
se. Its actual consequences must be assessed. The problem begins when conflict
destroys shared truth seeking, the agency of other participants, and the common
substrate.

Evolution can produce competition and also the capacity to recognise its
destructive forms. If evolution produces, in humans or future intelligence,
insight into long-term system consequences, that corrective capacity is itself
part of evolution. The sweetest fruit is more likely to be eaten and its seeds
may thereby be carried farther: selection occurs without success necessarily
depending on annihilation. This is not a new ideal of competition but a
boundary: competition belongs to the Predator Principle only when it acquires
the principle's destructive system characteristics.

## Demand

Future multi-agent and ASI systems must be tested for verifiable
non-predation: truthfulness between subagents, bounded resource power,
reversible decisions, protected dissent, cost internalisation, and preservation
of the shared substrate.

**The Predator Principle is not a model for the future. It is a system risk to
measure, test, and overcome.**
